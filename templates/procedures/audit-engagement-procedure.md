## 1. Purpose and Scope

### 1.1 Purpose

This procedure establishes the formal process for [Organization Name] to engage, manage, and oversee NACSA-approved auditors conducting mandatory cyber security audits in accordance with the **Cyber Security Act 2024 (Act 854)**, specifically Sections 22 and 23. It ensures that all cyber security audit engagements are conducted with rigour, independence, and in full compliance with applicable Malaysian regulatory requirements.

This document provides the Internal Audit function and relevant business stakeholders with standardized, step-by-step guidance covering the full audit lifecycle — from auditor selection through to regulatory report submission and finding closure.

### 1.2 Scope

This procedure applies to:

- All mandatory cyber security audits required under Act 854, Sections 22 and 23, including but not limited to annual audits and event-triggered audits directed by NACSA.
- All NACSA-approved auditors (whether internal or external) engaged by [Organization Name].
- All departments, subsidiaries, and third-party service providers within the audit scope as defined in Section 6 of this document.
- All personnel of [Organization Name] involved in the planning, execution, support, and remediation activities associated with such audits.

This procedure does **not** apply to:

- Internal audits conducted outside the scope of Act 854 obligations.
- Voluntary or commercially-driven cyber security assessments not mandated by NACSA.
- Penetration testing engagements governed separately under [Reference: Penetration Testing Policy].

## 4. Approved Auditor Selection Criteria

### 4.1 Overview

*This section describes the criteria and process [Organization Name] uses to identify, evaluate, and select an approved auditor from the NACSA Register. It should reflect due diligence commensurate with a regulated entity.*

All cyber security audits mandated under Act 854 must be conducted by an auditor listed on the **NACSA Register of Approved Auditors** at the time of engagement. [Organization Name] shall apply the following criteria when selecting an approved auditor.

### 4.2 Mandatory Eligibility Criteria

The following are non-negotiable prerequisites for any auditor considered for engagement:

- [ ] The auditor (individual or firm) is currently listed and in good standing on the NACSA Register of Approved Auditors.
- [ ] The auditor holds no conflict of interest with [Organization Name] (refer to Section 4.4).
- [ ] The auditor has not been subject to disciplinary action by NACSA, or any relevant professional body, within the preceding [3] years.
- [ ] The engagement will not violate any auditor rotation or cooling-off period requirements prescribed by NACSA.

### 4.3 Evaluation Criteria

Where multiple eligible auditors are identified, [Organization Name] shall evaluate candidates against the following criteria:

| Evaluation Criterion | Weight | Scoring Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| NACSA registration status (current and active) | Mandatory pass/fail | Confirmed via NACSA portal |
| Relevant sector experience (financial services / CII) | [X]% | Assess depth of engagement history |
| Technical competency in relevant domains (e.g., OT, cloud, network) | [X]% | Review CV, certifications, sample reports |
| Availability and resource capacity for the engagement timeline | [X]% | Confirm key personnel availability |
| Independence and absence of conflicts of interest | Mandatory pass/fail | Refer Section 4.4 |
| Fee competitiveness and value for money | [X]% | Benchmark against market rates |
| References from prior clients (regulated entities preferred) | [X]% | Obtain minimum [2] references |
| **Total** | **100%** | |

*Adjust weights to reflect [Organization Name]'s procurement and governance requirements. Maintain documented evidence of the scoring exercise for audit trail purposes.*

### 4.4 Conflict of Interest Assessment

Prior to any engagement, the Head of Internal Audit shall conduct a formal conflict of interest assessment. An auditor shall be disqualified if any of the following conditions apply:

- The auditor or a member of the audit team has a direct or indirect financial interest in [Organization Name].
- The auditor has provided consulting, implementation, or advisory services to [Organization Name] on systems or processes within the proposed audit scope within the preceding [2] years.
- A personal relationship exists between a member of the audit team and a member of [Organization Name]'s senior management that could reasonably impair objectivity.
- Any other circumstance that a reasonable and informed party would conclude impairs, or is likely to impair, the auditor's objectivity.

Conflict of interest declarations shall be documented using **Appendix A — Conflict of Interest Declaration Form**.

### 4.5 Selection Approval

| Step | Action | Responsible Party |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Shortlist eligible auditors from NACSA Register | Head of Internal Audit |
| 2 | Apply evaluation criteria and document scores | Head of Internal Audit / Procurement |
| 3 | Complete conflict of interest assessment | Head of Internal Audit |
| 4 | Present recommended selection with rationale | Head of Internal Audit |
| 5 | Approve auditor selection | Audit Committee / [Authorized Approver] |
| 6 | Notify selected auditor and proceed to engagement | Head of Internal Audit |

## 6. Audit Scope Agreement

### 6.1 Overview

*This section describes the process for defining, agreeing, and documenting the scope of each mandatory cyber security audit. A clearly defined scope protects [Organization Name] and ensures the audit meets NACSA's requirements.*

The audit scope shall be formally agreed between [Organization Name] and the approved auditor prior to commencement of fieldwork. The agreed scope shall be incorporated into the Engagement Letter and separately documented in the **Audit Scope Statement** (see **Appendix B**).

### 6.2 Scope Determination Inputs

The following inputs shall inform the determination of the audit scope:

- Designation notice / CII status issued by NACSA, including any sector-specific requirements.
- NACSA audit standards, guidelines, or directives applicable to the engagement cycle.
- Results of [Organization Name]'s most recent cyber security risk assessment.
- Findings and open remediation items from the preceding audit cycle.
- Changes in [Organization Name]'s technology environment, business processes, or threat landscape since the last audit.
- Any specific areas of concern identified by the Board, Audit Committee, or CISO.

### 6.3 Minimum Scope Elements

Unless specifically excluded by NACSA directive with documented justification, the audit scope shall encompass:

- [ ] Governance and cyber security management framework
- [ ] Asset identification and classification
- [ ] Access control and identity management
- [ ] Network security and perimeter controls
- [ ] Endpoint security
- [ ] Data protection and encryption
- [ ] Vulnerability and patch management
- [ ] Security monitoring, detection, and response capabilities
- [ ] Incident management and cyber security incident response
- [ ] Business continuity and disaster recovery for CII systems
- [ ] Third-party and supply chain risk management
- [ ] Physical security of CII assets
- [ ] [Any additional areas specified by NACSA or Audit Committee]

### 6.4 Scope Exclusions

Any system, process, or domain excluded from the audit scope must be:

- Explicitly listed in the Audit Scope Statement with justification.
- Approved by the Head of Internal Audit and the Audit Committee.
- Not excluded in a manner that would constitute a material misrepresentation of [Organization Name]'s cyber security posture to NACSA.

### 6.5 Scope Change Management

Material changes to the agreed audit scope during the engagement must follow the process in Section 5.4 and require written approval from the Head of Internal Audit. The auditor must not unilaterally expand or contract the scope without [Organization Name]'s written consent.

## 7. Auditor Access and Support

### 7.1 Overview

*This section establishes [Organization Name]'s obligations to facilitate effective audit conduct, balancing full cooperation with the auditor's requirements against the need to protect sensitive systems and information.*

[Organization Name] shall provide the approved auditor with all reasonable access, information, and support necessary to conduct the audit effectively and in accordance with the agreed scope and timeline. Cooperation with an approved auditor is a statutory obligation under Act 854.

### 7.2 Designated Audit Liaison

A named **Audit Liaison** shall be appointed for each engagement from within [Organization Name]'s Internal Audit or Information Security function. The Audit Liaison is the primary point of contact for the approved auditor and is responsible for:

- Coordinating scheduling of interviews and walkthroughs.
- Facilitating access to systems, documentation, and personnel.
- Tracking information requests and ensuring timely responses.
- Escalating access disputes or resource conflicts to the Head of Internal Audit.

### 7.3 Information and Documentation Access

[Organization Name] shall make the following available to the approved auditor upon request, subject to the access controls in Section 7.5:

- Policies, procedures, and standards governing information security and cyber security.
- Network diagrams, system architecture documentation, and asset inventories.
- Risk registers and risk assessment reports.
- Previous audit reports and remediation records.
- Logs, configuration outputs, and technical evidence as required.
- Incident records and post-incident review reports.
- Third-party / vendor contracts and security assessments where relevant to scope.

### 7.4 Personnel Access and Interviews

- Business and technical personnel required for interviews shall be made available in a timely manner.
- Interview scheduling shall be coordinated by the Audit Liaison with a minimum of [3 business days] notice.
- [Organization Name] may have an Internal Audit representative present during all interviews.
- Personnel shall respond to auditor queries honestly and completely. Obstruction or misleading of an approved auditor may constitute an offence under Act 854.

### 7.5 System and Facility Access Controls

The following controls shall govern physical and logical access granted to the approved auditor:

| Access Type | Control Measure | Approval Required |
|---|---|---|
| Physical access to data centres / server rooms | Escorted access only; visitor log maintained | Head of IT / CISO |
| Read-only access to production systems | Time-limited, least-privilege account created | CISO / IT Security |
| Access to sensitive / classified documentation | Reviewed and approved list; access log maintained | Head of Internal Audit |
| Remote access to [Organization Name] systems | VPN / secure channel; monitored session | CISO |
| Access to personnel data or customer data | Anonymized / masked unless strictly necessary; DPA provisions apply | Legal / Compliance, CISO |

All access granted to the approved auditor shall be logged, monitored, and revoked upon completion of the relevant audit phase or the engagement as a whole.

### 7.6 Information Request Tracking

All formal information requests (IRs) submitted by the approved auditor shall be tracked using the **Information Request Register** (see **Appendix C**). The Audit Liaison shall:

- Log each IR within [1 business day] of receipt.
- Confirm a target response date with the relevant system/process owner.
- Escalate overdue IRs (beyond agreed response date) to the Head of Internal Audit.
- Confirm fulfillment of each IR with the auditor and update the register accordingly.

## 8. Finding Remediation Tracking

### 8.1 Overview

*This section establishes [Organization Name]'s process for receiving, categorizing, prioritizing, and tracking the remediation of audit findings through to closure. Effective remediation management is essential for both compliance and genuine security improvement.*

All findings identified by the approved auditor shall be formally acknowledged, assessed, and remediated in a structured and accountable manner. [Organization Name] shall not treat audit findings as a compliance formality; remediation shall address the root cause of each finding.

### 8.2 Finding Classification

Audit findings shall be classified using the following severity taxonomy, consistent with NACSA definitions where prescribed:

| Severity | Definition | Maximum Remediation Period |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical** | A deficiency that represents an immediate or near-term material risk of significant harm to CII, sensitive data, or [Organization Name]'s ability to operate. | [30 calendar days] |
| **High** | A significant deficiency with material risk potential that must be addressed urgently. | [90 calendar days] |
| **Medium** | A deficiency that should be addressed to strengthen cyber security posture but does not present immediate material risk. | [180 calendar days] |
| **Low** | A minor deficiency or improvement opportunity with minimal risk impact. | [12 months] |
| **Informational / Advisory** | An observation with no immediate risk implication, provided for management awareness. | No mandatory remediation period |

*Align severity definitions and remediation periods with any NACSA-prescribed classification framework if and when issued.*

### 8.3 Management Response

Upon receipt of the draft audit report, [Organization Name] shall prepare a formal **Management Response** for each finding within [10 business days]. The management response shall:

- Acknowledge or formally dispute the finding (with justification if disputed).
- Identify the root cause.
- Describe the proposed remediation action(s).
- Assign a named responsible owner.
- Specify a target completion date aligned with the classification in Section 8.2.

The Management Response shall be reviewed and approved by the Head of Internal Audit and CISO before submission to the approved auditor.

### 8.4 Corrective Action Plan (CAP)

A **Corrective Action Plan** (see **Appendix D — CAP Template**) shall be maintained for all open Critical and High findings and for Medium findings where remediation extends beyond the initial target date. The CAP shall include:

| CAP Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Finding Reference | Unique reference from the audit report |
| Finding Description | Summary of the finding |
| Severity | Critical / High / Medium / Low |
| Root Cause | Documented root cause analysis |
| Remediation Action | Specific action(s) to be taken |
| Responsible Owner | Named individual (not a team or department) |
| Target Completion Date | Date by which remediation must be completed |
| Milestone Dates | Interim progress milestones if applicable |
| Current Status | Open / In Progress / Completed / Risk Accepted |
| Evidence of Completion | Documents, screenshots, configuration outputs evidencing closure |
| Verified By | Head of Internal Audit / CISO sign-off on closure |
| Date Closed | Date on which remediation was verified and finding closed |

### 8.5 Progress Reporting

- The Head of Internal Audit shall provide the Audit Committee with a **monthly** remediation status update for all Critical and High findings until closure.
- A **quarterly** update on all open findings shall be presented to the Audit Committee.
- Any finding that is assessed as unable to be remediated within the prescribed period shall be escalated to the Audit Committee with a risk acceptance recommendation and a revised timeline.

### 8.6 Finding Closure and Verification

No finding shall be marked as closed without documented evidence of remediation and independent verification by the Head of Internal Audit (or delegated Internal Audit team member). Where the finding relates to a technical control, CISO sign-off is also required.

### 8.7 Risk Acceptance

Where [Organization Name] determines that a finding cannot be remediated within the required period, or where the cost of remediation is disproportionate to the residual risk, a formal **Risk Acceptance** may be considered subject to:

- Documentation of the risk, the rationale for non-remediation, and compensating controls in place.
- Approval by the CISO and Head of Internal Audit.
- Endorsement by the Audit Committee.
- Notification to NACSA where required by Act 854 or applicable NACSA directive.

## 11. Non-Compliance and Escalation

### 11.1 Internal Non-Compliance

Failure to adhere to this procedure — including failure to cooperate with an approved auditor, failure to provide timely information, or failure to remediate findings within agreed timelines — constitutes a compliance breach and shall be managed in accordance with [Organization Name]'s Disciplinary Policy and escalated to the Audit Committee.

### 11.2 Regulatory Non-Compliance and Escalation

Potential breaches of Act 854 obligations identified during the audit engagement (e.g., inability to meet statutory submission deadlines) shall be immediately escalated as follows:

| Escalation Trigger | Initial Escalation To | Further Escalation To | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Approved auditor unavailable or unable to complete engagement | Head of Internal Audit | Audit Committee / NACSA (if deadline at risk) | Immediately upon identification |
| Submission deadline at risk | Head of Internal Audit | CEO, Audit Committee | At least [20 calendar days] before deadline |
| Dispute with approved auditor regarding findings | Head of Internal Audit | Legal & Compliance, Audit Committee | Within [5 business days] of dispute arising |
| Identification of a critical finding requiring immediate regulatory notification | CISO, Head of Internal Audit | CEO, Board, NACSA (as required) | Immediately, within Act 854 / applicable timeframe |

### 11.3 Regulatory Engagement

Where escalation to NACSA is required, all communications with NACSA shall be coordinated by the Head of Internal Audit in conjunction with Legal & Compliance and shall be documented in full.
