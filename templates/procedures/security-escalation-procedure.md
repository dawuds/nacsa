## 1. Purpose and Scope

### 1.1 Purpose

*Describe the intent of this document and why it exists. Reference the regulatory obligation it satisfies.*

This procedure establishes the formal escalation framework for security alerts and events detected through the continuous monitoring of [Organization Name]'s National Critical Information Infrastructure (NCII) systems and assets. It defines the criteria, thresholds, communication channels, and responsibilities that govern how security events are triaged, escalated, and reported — from initial detection through to resolution or formal notification to NACSA.

This document fulfils the procedural obligations imposed upon [Organization Name] as a designated NCII operator under **Section 18** (Duties of NCII Sector Leads and NCII Entities) and **Section 23** (Notification of Cyber Security Incidents) of the **Cyber Security Act 2024 (Act 854)**.

### 1.2 Scope

*Define the boundaries of this procedure — which systems, teams, and event types are covered.*

This procedure applies to:

- **Systems in Scope:** All NCII-designated systems, networks, applications, and data assets operated or managed by [Organization Name], including on-premises infrastructure, co-location facilities, and cloud environments classified as NCII.
- **Personnel in Scope:** All Security Operations Centre (SOC) analysts, SOC Lead, IT security personnel, incident responders, and senior management of [Organization Name] who have a role in the detection, triage, escalation, or notification of cyber security events.
- **Event Types in Scope:** All security alerts, anomalies, and events generated by or reported to the SOC, including but not limited to alerts from SIEM platforms, endpoint detection and response (EDR) tools, network intrusion detection systems (NIDS), threat intelligence feeds, and manual reports from staff.
- **Third-Party Managed Services:** This procedure extends to any third-party Security Operations Centre or Managed Security Service Provider (MSSP) contracted by [Organization Name] to monitor NCII systems, who are bound by this procedure through contractual obligations.

### 1.3 Out of Scope

*List what this procedure does not cover to prevent ambiguity.*

- Security events affecting non-NCII systems that do not have the potential to impact NCII assets (handled under [Reference separate IT incident management procedure]).
- Physical security incidents unless they directly impact the integrity or availability of NCII systems.
- Routine IT service desk tickets that have not been assessed as security events.

## 4. Alert Classification Tiers

*Define the alert classification taxonomy used by the SOC. Each tier should have unambiguous criteria, target response times, and ownership. This section forms the foundation of the escalation framework. Tailor the criteria to your organisation's environment and risk appetite.*

### 4.1 Classification Framework Overview

[Organization Name] SOC classifies all security alerts into four (4) priority tiers based on the assessed impact, urgency, and nature of the event. Classification determines the response timeline and escalation path.

> All alerts generated by automated tooling shall be assigned an initial classification by the detecting tool. SOC analysts shall validate and may upgrade or downgrade the classification upon triage. Any upgrade to P1 must be immediately communicated to the SOC Lead.

### 4.2 Alert Priority Tier Definitions

| Priority Tier | Severity Label | Description | Examples | Initial Response Target | Escalation Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **P1** | Critical | An active or imminent cyber attack or compromise with confirmed or highly likely significant impact on NCII systems, data, or services. | Confirmed ransomware deployment; active data exfiltration from NCII systems; successful compromise of privileged accounts; critical infrastructure disruption. | **15 minutes** | Immediate escalation to SOC Lead and CISO. NACSA notification evaluation to commence within 1 hour. |
| **P2** | High | A serious security event with potential for significant impact if not contained promptly. Compromise is suspected but not yet confirmed. | Suspected lateral movement; multiple failed privileged account logins from anomalous locations; malware detected on NCII-connected endpoint; exploitation attempt against critical vulnerability. | **1 hour** | Escalate to SOC Lead within 30 minutes of detection. Management notification within 2 hours. |
| **P3** | Medium | A security event with moderate potential impact. Requires investigation but immediate containment is not required. | Phishing email delivery to multiple users; unusual outbound traffic to known suspicious IP; policy violation involving sensitive data; single failed privileged login from anomalous location. | **4 hours** | Escalate to Senior Analyst. SOC Lead notification during business hours or if event escalates. |
| **P4** | Low | Informational or low-risk alerts requiring monitoring and logging. No immediate action required beyond documentation. | Successful login from new device (user-confirmed); routine vulnerability scan findings; expired certificate alert; standard policy reminders. | **Next business day** | No escalation required unless status changes. Log and track in SOC ticketing system. |

**P1 — Critical Criteria:**

- Confirmed compromise of an NCII-designated system or the systems that directly support it.
- Active exfiltration of classified, sensitive, or personally identifiable information (PII) from NCII systems.
- Ransomware or destructive malware active within the NCII environment.
- Denial of service attack causing or likely to cause unavailability of critical NCII services.
- Compromise of privileged access credentials (domain admin, root, NCII system admin).
- [Add organisation-specific criteria]

**P2 — High Criteria:**

- Malware detected but not yet confirmed to have propagated.
- Indicators of compromise (IOCs) associated with known advanced persistent threat (APT) groups observed on NCII systems.
- Anomalous privileged access activity that cannot be explained by authorised change activity.
- Significant data loss or leakage risk identified but not yet confirmed as exfiltration.
- [Add organisation-specific criteria]

**P3 — Medium Criteria:**

- Security control failure (e.g., antivirus disabled, logging gap) on a non-critical system.

- Vulnerability with CVSS score ≥ 7.0 identified on a production system.
- [Add organisation-specific criteria]

## 5. Escalation Thresholds

*Define the specific conditions that trigger escalation at each tier. Thresholds must be objective and measurable to prevent ambiguity and ensure consistent application by all SOC analysts.*

### 5.1 Escalation Principles

The following principles govern all escalation decisions:

1. **Err on the side of escalation.** When an analyst is uncertain whether an event meets escalation criteria, the default action is to escalate rather than defer.
2. **Time-bound accountability.** Each escalation step has a defined time limit. Failure to receive acknowledgement within the defined window requires escalation to the next level.
3. **No single point of failure.** All escalation paths have a defined backup contact in the event the primary contact is unavailable.
4. **Documentation first.** All escalation actions must be logged in the SOC ticketing system before or immediately after the escalation communication is made.

### 5.2 Tier-Based Escalation Thresholds

| Trigger Condition | Classification | Escalation Action | Time Limit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Alert auto-generated by SIEM and unacknowledged | Any | SOC Analyst notified by ticketing system | 15 minutes |
| P1 alert generated (automated or manual) | P1 | SOC Analyst escalates to SOC Lead immediately | 15 minutes |
| P1 unacknowledged by SOC Lead | P1 | Escalate to CISO and Deputy SOC Lead | 30 minutes |
| P2 alert unresolved after initial triage | P2 | SOC Analyst escalates to Senior SOC Analyst | 30 minutes |
| P2 event confirmed as active compromise | P2 → P1 | Reclassify to P1 and initiate P1 escalation chain | Immediate |
| P3 alert unresolved within 4-hour response window | P3 | Escalate to SOC Lead for review | 4 hours |
| Any event assessed as potential NCII impact | Any | SOC Lead notified; NACSA notification evaluation initiated | Immediate upon assessment |
| Volume surge: >20 P3/P4 alerts in 1 hour from same source | P3/P4 | SOC Lead notified; assess for coordinated attack | 30 minutes |
| [Add organisation-specific thresholds] | [Tier] | [Action] | [Timeframe] |

## 7. Management Notification Triggers

*Define the specific conditions that require escalation beyond the SOC team to senior management. Management should receive structured, timely briefings that enable informed decision-making without operational overload.*

### 7.1 Notification Principles

Management notification is not equivalent to requesting management intervention. The purpose is to ensure that:
- Appropriate authority levels are aware of significant security events.
- Decisions requiring management authorisation (e.g., system shutdown, NACSA notification, public disclosure) can be made promptly.
- Management can fulfil their regulatory obligations under Act 854.

### 7.2 Mandatory Management Notification Triggers

The following events require mandatory management notification regardless of time of day:

| Trigger | Notification Recipients | Timeline | Notification Method |
|---|---|---|---|
| **P1 alert confirmed** | SOC Lead, CISO | Within **1 hour** of P1 classification | Phone call + Incident ticket |
| **P1 event involving confirmed data breach or exfiltration** | CISO, CEO/MD, Legal Counsel, DPO | Within **2 hours** of confirmation | Phone call + Secure email |
| **NCII system unavailability exceeding [30] minutes** | CISO, COO/CTO | Within **1 hour** of confirmed unavailability | Phone call + Incident ticket |
| **Confirmed compromise of privileged accounts** | CISO, Head of IT | Within **1 hour** of confirmation | Phone call + Incident ticket |
| **NACSA notification threshold met** | CISO, CEO/MD, Legal Counsel | Immediately upon threshold assessment | Phone call + Secure email |
| **Suspected APT / nation-state activity** | CISO, CEO/MD | Within **2 hours** of suspicion | Phone call + Secure email |
| **Ransomware confirmed active in environment** | CISO, CEO/MD, COO/CTO, Legal | Immediately upon confirmation | Phone call |

### 9.3 After-Hours Alert Response Procedure

*Provide a clear, step-by-step procedure that an on-call analyst can follow when responding to an alert after hours. This should be self-sufficient without needing to reference other systems.*

**Upon receiving an after-hours alert:**

1. **Acknowledge the alert** in the SOC ticketing system within [15] minutes of notification.
2. **Assess the alert** using available tools ([SIEM platform / EDR / NIDS]) to determine initial classification.
3. **Apply the classification tiers** defined in Section 4 to assign a priority.
4. **If P4 or P3 (below escalation threshold):**
   - Log the alert, assign to the next business day queue.
   - Document initial assessment and any immediate observations.
   - Continue monitoring. Reassess if further related alerts are received.
5. **If P3 (meeting escalation threshold) or P2:**
   - Contact On-Call SOC Lead via the escalation sequence in Section 6.4.
   - Begin initial containment steps if authorised to do so (see Appendix B — Pre-Authorised Containment Actions).
   - Create a formal incident ticket and begin incident timeline documentation.
6. **If P1:**
   - Immediately contact On-Call SOC Lead via phone.
   - Contact On-Call CISO within 30 minutes if SOC Lead authorises management notification.
   - Initiate the Incident Response Plan ([IR Plan Document ID]).
   - Do not wait for confirmation of compromise before escalating — escalate on reasonable suspicion.
7. **Document all actions** with precise timestamps in the incident ticket.
8. **Prepare a written handover** summary for the incoming shift, including status, actions taken, and outstanding tasks.

*Define the approved communication channels for after-hours incident communication. Do not use unapproved or insecure channels for sensitive incident details.*

### 9.5 After-Hours Shift Handover

At the conclusion of the after-hours period, the on-call analyst must:

- Complete a written handover note in the SOC ticketing system covering all alerts handled, actions taken, and any open incidents.
- Verbally brief the incoming SOC team lead or senior analyst at the start of business hours.
- Ensure all P1 or P2 incidents are formally handed over with acknowledgement from the receiving analyst.
- Flag any alerts that were downgraded but may warrant follow-up review.
